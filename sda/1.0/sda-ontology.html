Description

The Scholarly Domain Activities Ontology (SDA) defines a controlled vocabulary of common scholarly activities. It is adapted from the project Modeling the Scholarly Domain for virtual research environments.

This ontology redefines and extends the motivations described in the W3C Web Annotation Data Model for a broader range of digital research practices.

Terms

  • assemblinga oa:motivation

    Putting any kind of elements from the corpus together to form a workset which can be shared, published, disseminated, or referred to by another annotation. May be an iterative or continuous process based on or involving other activities such as sampling, observing, or evidencing.

  • assessinga oa:motivation

    Determining the quality of an object of interest or information in terms of its relevance or utility to a corpus or project, or in terms of its provenance or perceived quality.

  • commentinga oa:motivation

    Making complex statements or presenting arguments to explain observations. Comments can also present historical or analytical reflections informed by academic research, including pointers to external sources and references.

  • comparinga oa:motivation

    Noting the differences between elements in terms of their structural and conceptual features.

  • contextualisinga oa:motivation

    Creating relationships between corpus elements and referential elements necessary or relevant for interpretation. Considered a specific type of referring.

  • describinga oa:motivation

    To characterise something according to its structural or conceptual features. Encompasses a broad range of activities, from making simple factual statements to interpretive practices such as commenting and comparing.

  • evidencinga oa:motivation

    Indicates an element is intended to serve as evidence for a scholarly claim or argument.

  • illustratinga oa:motivation

    Visual content intended to express an idea, argument, or description that is represented by text, speech, numeric data, or by other visual elements.

  • notetakinga oa:motivation

    Jotting down thoughts, remarks, or notes at any stage of the working and research process.

  • observinga oa:motivation

    Labelling or identifying a feature of an element with remarks that aim to be simple and objective, using conventional terminology where possible to facilitate reuse. A narrower category than describing, calling for a specific empirically grounded stance.

  • referringa oa:motivation

    Pointing to an external or internal resource, for example by adding a hypertext link or citation.

  • samplinga oa:motivation

    A subtype of selection that constitutes a new corpus (the sample) as a subset of the original corpus. Unlike general selection, sampling is always performed on an existing corpus.

  • selectinga oa:motivation

    Selecting objects of interest or information from the corpus based on certain criteria, to form a new corpus or workset or to add resources to an existing one.

  • translatinga oa:motivation

    Converting and interpreting terminology, concepts, theories, etc. for oneself or for different disciplinary audiences.
